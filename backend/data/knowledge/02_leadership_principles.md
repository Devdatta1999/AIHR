# Nimbus Labs — Leadership Principles

These six principles guide how we work, who we promote, and how we evaluate candidates. Every interview should probe against at least two of these.

## 1. Bias for action
Speed matters. Most decisions are reversible — make them quickly and iterate. We would rather see a rough solution in a PR by Friday than a beautiful doc by next Thursday. Waiting for perfect information is itself a decision, and usually the wrong one.

**Interview signal:** Ask about a time the candidate moved without full clarity. Look for framing: "I had ~60% of the info, so I…" rather than "I waited until I had consensus."

## 2. Own the outcome
You are accountable for the thing working, not just the thing shipping. That means testing the thing in production, watching the dashboard, following up with the customer who hit the edge case, writing the postmortem. Ownership doesn't end at the merge button.

**Interview signal:** Ask the candidate to walk through a launch end-to-end. Does the story include what happened *after* the launch? Did they instrument it? Did they hit a regression and own the fix?

## 3. Think in systems
A feature is a system — code, data, on-call runbook, customer docs, metrics. A team is a system. A company is a system. We promote engineers who zoom out before they zoom in: who understand why the feature exists, who uses it, and what fails when it breaks.

**Interview signal:** Present a design question. Does the candidate start with requirements, users, and failure modes — or immediately draw boxes? Push them to identify a single point of failure in their own design.

## 4. Disagree and commit (and revisit)
Once the decision is made, we move together — even if we voted against it. But we mark the decision and revisit it when new data arrives. Disagree silently and resist quietly is a fireable offense; disagreeing loudly then executing loyally is the ideal.

**Interview signal:** Ask about a time the candidate lost an argument. Did they sabotage the work afterward, work around it, or actually commit? Did they later bring data to revisit it?

## 5. Be candid, kindly
Give feedback that's specific, actionable, and on-time. Protecting someone from hard truth isn't kindness — it's avoidance. We assume good intent, we don't get personal, but we don't pull punches either.

**Interview signal:** Ask about a tough piece of feedback the candidate gave. Was it specific? Did the relationship survive? What did the receiving person actually do with it?

## 6. Hire people better than you
The bar goes up every hire. If you would not learn something from this candidate, don't hire them. Mediocre hires are a tax on the company for years — we'd rather leave a role open for three months than fill it with a "just okay" hire.

**Interview signal:** At debrief, every interviewer should be able to say "I'd learn X from this person." If no one can, it's a no.

## How we map these to interview question types
- **Culture fit:** Values, LP #5 (candor), LP #6 (raise the bar)
- **Leadership:** LP #2 (ownership), LP #4 (disagree-commit), LP #6 (hiring)
- **Situational / judgment:** LP #1 (bias for action), LP #3 (systems thinking), LP #4 (revisit)
- **Technical deep-dive:** LP #2 (outcome), LP #3 (systems), plus role-specific stack questions
